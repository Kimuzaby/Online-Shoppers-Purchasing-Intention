Online Shoppers Purchasing Intention

Classification Analysis

Author

Erick Xavier Maldonado

1. Business Understanding

In the e-commerce sector, marketing budgets are often wasted on “window shoppers”, users who browse without intent to purchase. The following analysis focuses on a dataset of 12,330 distinct user sessions over a one-year period. By ensuring each session belongs to a different user, we avoid biases related to specific campaigns or user profiles.

Currently, the vast majority of sessions representing a 84.5% do not result in a transaction. Treating all visitors equally leads to inefficient resource allocation. The marketing team aims to shift from a “spray and pray” strategy to a precision-based approach, targeting only those with a high probability of conversion.

Research Questions:

  1. Behavioral Intent: Can we accurately distinguish between a “bouncer” and a “buyer” using engagement metrics like PageValues and ExitRates?

  2. The “Special Day” Effect: Does the proximity to a special event, like Valentine’s Day for example, significantly alter purchasing probability, justifying specific holiday campaigns?

2. Data Understanding and Preparation

The dataset online_shoppers_intention_classification.csv sourced from the UCI Machine Learning Repository contains 12,330 observations.

Dataset Characteristics:

  • Imbalance: 10,422 (84.5%) sessions are Negative (No Purchase), while 1,908 (15.5%) are Positive (Purchase).

  • Features: There are 10 numerical attributes (including Google Analytics metrics) and 8 categorical/binary attributes.

  • Missing Values: The dataset contains no missing values.

We will train three models. Given the 15.5% positive class rate, we will apply Class Weights to the Decision Tree and Random Forest to penalize misclassification of the minority class.

Model Fit Statistic: AIC = 4412.51

The model was fitted using all available predictors, AIC = 4412.51. While some individual categorical levels showed lower significance, we retained the full feature set (no trimming) to preserve the interpretability of all Traffic Sources and Months for the business stakeholders.

  • Friction is the Enemy: The most significant finding is the extreme negative impact of ExitRates, Odds Ratio \(\approx\) 0.0001 and BounceRates, Odds Ratio \(\approx\) 0.0048. This statistically confirms that users who demonstrate “leaving behavior” or single-page sessions are virtually guaranteed not to buy.

  • The “Special Day” Effect: Addressing Research Question 2, the SpecialDay variable has an Odds Ratio of 1.16911. This indicates that for every unit increase in closeness to a special holiday, the odds of a purchase increase by approximately 16.9%, holding everything else constant.

  • Seasonality: Seasonality plays a measurable role, for example, July with a OR = 1.18 shows a higher likelihood of conversion, while February, OR = 0.15 shows a significantly lower likelihood compared to the baseline.

Model 2: Decision Tree (CART)

We include weights = model_weights to force the tree to prioritize the 1,908 positive cases.
The Decision Tree simplifies the user journey into hierarchical rules.

The tree was optimized using cross-validation. he final selected model used a CP of 0.00599, which balanced tree depth, which balanced tree depth against predictive accuracy.

  • The “Golden Rule” of Page Value: The root node split confirms that log_page_values is the single most important discriminator. The tree splits first on whether log_page_values is less than 0.032.

  • The “Window Shopper” Path: If a user has a low Page Value, left branch, the model immediately categorizes them into a node where the probability of purchase drops to 10% or less.

  • The “Buyer” Path: If the Page Value is high (right branch), the model identifies a high-intent segment where the probability of purchase jumps to 46% or higher.

In order to select the best model for deployment, we compared the three models using Threshold Tuning to maximize the F1 Score.

  • We have a Winner: The Random Forest model is selected as the champion. It achieved the highest F1 Score (0.671), indicating it provides the best balance between catching true buyers and avoiding false alarms.

Performance Metrics:

  • Discriminatory Power: The Random Forest achieved an AUC of 0.919, comparable to the Logistic Regression’s 0.924. This confirms the model is excellent at ranking customers from “most likely” to “least likely” to buy.

  • Sensitivity: With a Sensitivity of 0.815, the model successfully identifies 81.5% of all actual buyers.

  • Rationale: Although Logistic Regression had a marginally higher AUC, the Random Forest demonstrated a better F1 score. This suggests it is more robust at handling the complex interactions between categorical variables, like TrafficType and Region without overfitting.

Cumulative Gains Chart (Best Model)

The Random Forest typically yields the best AUC. We plot the Gains chart to visualize the “Lift” provided by the Google Analytics metrics.

  • The blue model line rises extremely sharply at the beginning. This indicates that the customers the model ranked as “highest probability” are the actual buyers.

  • By targeting approximately the top 1,000 cases, roughly about the top 30% of the test population, the model captures nearly 90% of all available conversions, where the curve begins to flatten.

  • The “flat” part of the blue curve represents the majority of site visitors who are “window shoppers.” Retargeting these users wastes budget because they do not contribute. The chart proves we can safely stop marketing to the bottom 70% of visitors while still capturing the vast majority of revenue.

6. Deployment and Recommendations

Narrative and Recommendations

Based on the Cumulative Gains analysis and the insights that we got from the models, we propose the following actions:

  • Efficiency Strategy (Stop “Spray and Pray”): The Cumulative Gains chart displays a steep curve where the top 20% of sessions (ranked by the model) account for the vast majority of conversions.

  • Recommendation: We can cut retargeting ad spend by ignoring the bottom 80% of visitors while still retaining the majority of revenue. Marketing resources should be concentrated solely on the top 2 deciles.

“Special Day” Campaigns:

  • Recommendation: Given the 16.9% increase in purchase odds near holidays identified by the Logistic Regression, the marketing team should launch aggressive, short-term campaigns in the window leading up to major holidays. The data proves users are primed to buy during these periods.

Real-Time “Friction” Intervention:

  • Recommendation: Since PageValues is a real-time metric, we should deploy the model directly on the website. If a user’s score crosses the optimal threshold but they display “exit behavior” the site should trigger an immediate “Save the Sale” popup, like free shipping. This targets high-intent users at the exact moment of friction.
